The Book

Synopsis

Paramanta is the third book of the Wildlife Trilogy — and the most defiant. Where Wildlife excavates the wound and Archeus translates it into myth, Paramanta is the practice that emerges from both. It is a moving meditation on the art of glide. It is the answer the body found when the mind had run out of answers.

The word Paramanta holds the whole philosophy inside it. Paracelsus — the 500 year old Swiss alchemist who walked out into nature because nature was the only book worth reading. Paramahansa — the supreme swan, the highest state of consciousness, the soul that moves through the world without being disturbed by it. And the Manta Ray — the master of glide, the teacher, the creature that has solved the problem of effortless movement through a resistant medium.

The book is built around one central question that the author asks himself every morning when he carries his foil down to the dock on Lac Léman: what does it mean to move through the world without resistance? Not the physical question only — though the physics are real and the technique is taught honestly and in full. The deeper question. What does it mean to live without the armour? To inhabit the body with elegance and power. To stop fighting what you are and start becoming it completely.

Paramanta is part technique manual, part philosophy, part memoir, part sea shanty. It moves between the dock and the interior life, between the practical and the mythic, between the forty four year old man who failed a thousand times before he flew and the eight year old boy running through mustard fields in Lincolnshire with a dog called Scamper who was always already searching for the glide without knowing its name.

The problem is the solution. That's the book's central teaching. And the proof is a life fully and honestly lived — from Grimsby to Geneva, from the worm in Fiji to the bioluminescence in the deep, from the dock start to the breach — arriving finally at seventy five centimetres above the water, travelling at twenty five kilometres an hour, in a state of effortless grace.

This is not a book about pump foiling. It is a book about what happens when a human being finally stops fighting their own nature and learns to glide.

"On cherche le glisse. We search for the glide.
This is not a destination. It is a practice.
The problem is the solution."

The Structure

Three Threads, One Voice

Every chapter of Paramanta weaves three threads simultaneously. They are never separate. The technique always opens into philosophy. The philosophy always grounds in the personal. The personal always returns to the water.

The Technique
How to pump foil. Honestly, physically, without mystification. The dock start. The jump. The landing. The pump. The gaze. The thousand falls. Written for the body not the mind.
The Philosophy
What the practice teaches beyond the practice. Paracelsus. The nudibranch lens. Grit to pearl. The stone effect. The drum. The art of glide as a way of inhabiting a life.
The Personal
The life that brought him to the dock. The illness. The wife's cancer. The body breaking down. The post-exertion malaise. The man who needed the water to teach him what no person could.

From Dock to Breach

Context

Where This Book Lives

Paramanta sits in the tradition of books that use a physical practice as a philosophical lens. Eugen Herrigel's Zen in the Art of Archery. Robert Pirsig's Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance. Norman Maclean's A River Runs Through It. Books where the activity is never really about the activity.

It also draws from the nature writing tradition of Robert Macfarlane and the mythic storytelling of Martin Shaw — grounded in landscape, alive to the symbolic layer, unafraid of the deep.

But it is finally its own thing. A Grimsby boy who ended up on an Alpine lake and found in the art of glide the philosophy his life had been building toward since he was eight years old with a white jack russell called Scamper and a yellow BMX with blue wheels and mustard fields stretching out to the horizon.

It is the book he wishes he had read at eighteen.

Which means it is exactly the book the world needs now.
