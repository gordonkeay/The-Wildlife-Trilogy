00 · Why This Exists

An invitation to
something more

Most of us are already trying. We lace up our running shoes because we want to be healthy — and running is free, accessible, and for many people a genuine source of joy and mental clarity. We join a CrossFit class or a gym because we want to feel strong, and because something in us recognises that doing hard things alongside other people feels fundamentally better than doing them alone. We work hard because we want to contribute, to build something, to feel that what we do has value.

These instincts are exactly right. The desire to move, to belong, to do work that matters — these are among the most human things there are. This project is not a critique of any of that. It is an addition to it. Another door, left open.

The reality is that many of us are still looking for something we haven't quite found yet. Around 70% of regular runners experience injuries that interrupt their practice — not because they're doing something wrong, but because the body eventually asks for variety, for softness, for water instead of pavement. High-intensity training, loved by millions for the community and the challenge it creates, can sometimes leave the body asking for restoration as much as effort. And for all the fitness options available to us, genuine outdoor community — the kind where people of different ages and backgrounds build something real together — remains surprisingly rare.

"The missing piece, almost always, is community. People don't join CrossFit for the workouts alone — they join for the belonging. They want to feel good, to be seen, to share something real with other people. We want that for everyone, on the water and in the workshop, for as long as their body wants to move." — Gordon Keay, Founder

The Pump Foil Lab is a place where those needs are met — through a practice that is low-impact and lifelong, a workshop where things are made and repaired together, and a community that is genuinely open to all. Something that sits alongside whatever else you love, and quietly makes everything better.

It responds, with care and intention, to several things our time is asking of us.

01 · Essence

What is this place?

The Pump Foil Lab is an open, intergenerational workshop on the water's edge — a living laboratory where biomimicry, craft, community, and movement practice converge. It is not a conventional business, and not a conventional school. It is a dojo in the deepest sense: a place where mastery is pursued together, where things are made and repaired by hand, where the lake is both classroom and medicine — and where everyone is welcome from the very first day.

At its heart is a simple and joyful question: what can fish, birds, whales and water itself teach us about moving through it? The answers become foils, wings, hulls — and the practices that bring them alive.

Open doors. Open innovation. The lab belongs to those who show up — beginner or expert, young or old, designer or poet, runner or sailor or complete newcomer. All bring something. All leave with more than they came with.

02 · The Practice

Movement as inquiry — and medicine

Pump foiling is one of the most biomimetic movement practices humans have yet invented. To pump a foil is to move as a manta ray moves, as a dolphin moves — oscillating through a fluid medium, extracting energy from the water itself. It requires presence, sensitivity, and an embodied understanding of fluid dynamics.

It is also one of the most sustainable fitness practices available to us. Running is wonderful — and for many people it will always be part of life. But running on pavement places real demands on joints that accumulate over time, and around 70% of regular runners experience injuries that interrupt their practice at some point. Not because they're doing something wrong — but because the body eventually asks for variety. For something soft. For water instead of concrete.

CrossFit and high-intensity training have given millions of people something genuinely valuable: community, challenge, and the deep satisfaction of doing hard things alongside others. The community is the whole point — and that instinct is exactly right. What the body sometimes also needs, alongside intensity, is restoration. Movement that builds without breaking down. A practice that asks for presence rather than maximum effort.

Pump foiling offers something that sits beautifully alongside both. It is full-body, cardiovascular, and strength-building — and completely low impact. Water carries you. The movement is fluid, oscillating, rhythmic. Joints are mobilised rather than hammered. It runs beside running, complements strength training, or stands entirely on its own. And it can be sustained across decades — not abandoned at fifty when the knees finally say enough.

Stress, restoration — and the quiet of the water

Many of us carry sustained pressure in our bodies — the kind that accumulates over months and years, that exercise alone doesn't always resolve. Rhythmic, bilateral movement in natural environments is one of the most well-documented interventions for nervous system recovery and cortisol regulation. The body finds a pattern; the mind follows.

On a foil, this state arrives naturally and completely. The balance required demands full presence, which means the ruminating mind finally gets to rest. It is, in the most literal sense, a moving meditation. And it costs nothing except showing up to the water.

"Most people who come to the water for sport discover, after a while, that they came for something else too. A quietness. A sense of being fully in their body. Something that the gym and the busy week couldn't quite reach. That is what we are making a home for." — Gordon Keay, Founder
"This project was born from a breakdown and a recovery. Years of overtraining, chronic illness, and the weight of caring for a seriously ill partner and two young children brought me to a place where pushing harder was no longer possible. The lake taught me what nothing else had — to listen to my body, to find presence instead of performance, to let movement heal rather than deplete. That is what this lab is for — and that is who it is for." — Gordon Keay, Founder, Genthod, Lac Léman

Safety: learning together, carefully

We must be honest: pump foiling is not without danger. The foil is hard and sharp. Falls happen. The first sessions require respect, care, and proper instruction. The lab takes this with complete seriousness — it is the foundation on which everything else is built. A beginner is never alone on the water. Progression is gradual and guided. Safety protocols are developed collectively and improved continuously. Learning to fall safely comes before learning to fly.

Never Alone

All beginner and early intermediate sessions are accompanied. No one enters the water without a spotter or coach present.

Progressive Pathways

Every member follows a structured learning pathway — understanding the equipment before using it, mastering fundamentals before advancing.

Collective Protocols

Safety practices are developed, tested, and refined by the community itself. Not imposed from above — built from experience and shared openly.

Protective Equipment

We design and test protective gear as part of the lab's R&D programme — biomimetically informed, bioregionally sourced where possible.

04 · Design Methodology

From lake to hand —
the four stages

At the heart of the lab's design programme is a simple cycle — one that moves from careful observation of the natural world to a finished object shaped by human hands. It is both a research methodology and a making practice, and it can be followed by a curious twelve-year-old or an experienced engineer with equal reward.

👁

01 · Observe

Study the species of Lac Léman — the grebe, the fera, the pike, the eel. Understand how they move, how they generate lift and thrust, how their bodies are shaped by millions of years of fluid dynamics. Nature has already solved the problem. We begin by looking.

📐

02 · Model

Translate the biological geometry into a digital foil profile. Design the wing shape that embodies what the species taught us. Then 3D print it as a precise prototype core — rapid, repeatable, testable. The machine serves the idea.

🌊

03 · Test

Laminate the printed core in natural fibre and bio-resin. Take it to the lake. Feel what works. Document everything. Refine the shape, print again, test again. Iterate until the geometry sings — until the foil moves the way the species moves.

🪵

04 · Translate

Take the proven geometry and hand-build it in wood and flax — the definitive object. A foil whose shape was inspired by a fish that lives in this lake, built from materials that grew near its shores, shaped by hands in a workshop on its edge. That object has a story a production foil can never tell.

"The 3D printer finds the shape. The hands make it real. One is in service of the other — and both are in service of the lake." — Gordon Keay, Founder

Every design the lab develops is published openly — both the digital file for those who want to print and test, and the making guide for those who want to build by hand. Anyone in the world with a 3D printer and some flax cloth can prototype a Léman-inspired foil. Anyone with patience and good tools can build the real thing. The knowledge belongs to everyone.

04 · The Model

A different way of doing things

Most sports equipment models follow the same logic: buy, use, discard, replace. It is expensive, wasteful, and creates barriers to entry that exclude the very people who might benefit most from the practice. We are building something different — something where the equipment, the knowledge, and the community belong to everyone who shows up.

The Collective Subscription Model

Foils belong to the club, not to individuals. Members subscribe to access equipment, participate in workshops, and contribute to the continuous improvement of shared tools.

Every repair is a lesson. Every modification is documented. The equipment becomes a living archive of collective learning — improving over time through the hands and experience of the whole community.

This lowers the cost barrier to entry, deepens engagement with the tools, and creates a fundamentally different relationship between people and the objects they use. You don't just ride it. You understand it. You care for it. You make it better.

The ateliers sit at the heart of this model — not just making sessions, but repair evenings, maintenance mornings, open-door workshops where anyone can come and work on anything. Where an elder who has worked wood all their life teaches a teenager how to sand a surface. Where an engineer explains to a child why a dolphin's fin reduces drag. Where strangers become collaborators over something that needed fixing. And then everyone takes it to the lake and tests it together.

What happens here, day to day

Design & Shape

Hands-on foil shaping using bioregional materials. Biomimetically inspired. Iterative, documented, open-source.

Repair & Maintain

Weekly atelier sessions focused on care of shared equipment. Every member learns the tools. Repair is practice, not admin.

Water Sessions

Guided and open sessions on the lake. All levels, all ages. Safety-first culture. Regular community rides at dawn and dusk.

Biomimicry Study

Observing the Léman's species — grebe, pike, fera, eel — and translating their mechanics directly into prototype designs.

Coaching & Mentorship

Experienced riders sharing skills across generations. Structured progression pathways. Radical patience. No gatekeeping.

Wellbeing Sessions

Guided sessions weaving movement practice with breath, presence and nervous system awareness. For those who come to the water for more than sport.

Open Making Nights

Evenings where the doors open, tools come out, and anyone can work on anything. The community is the curriculum.

Testing Days

Dedicated days on the water to test new designs, compare iterations, gather data. Rigorous and joyful in equal measure.

06 · Principles

How we work — the dojo code

  • Everyone is welcome. Age, ability, background and fitness level are not filters. The lab belongs to anyone willing to show up with curiosity and care — the complete beginner as much as the experienced rider.

  • Community is the point. The foiling is the reason people arrive. The community is the reason they stay. We build relationships alongside foils — and we take both equally seriously.

  • Nature is the senior designer. We observe before we invent. Every foil geometry begins with a species, a behaviour, a flow pattern in the natural world.

  • The lake is the teacher. Not a backdrop. Not a venue. The lake is the reason — ecologically, physiologically, spiritually. We go to it with reverence.

  • Repair before replace. An object repaired with care is more valuable than a new one. The act of repair teaches. The culture of repair sustains.

  • Safety is care, not fear. We take the risks of this practice seriously — and we build a culture of mutual care that makes it accessible to all. Learning to fall safely is the first lesson.

  • Movement is medicine. We build for longevity, not performance. The body sustained across decades matters more than the body maximised for one season. Low impact. High joy.

  • Open by default. Designs are shared. Failures are shared. Knowledge flows out as freely as it flows in. We don't hoard insight.

  • The place shapes the making. We use what grows nearby. A creative constraint that connects us to landscape and season — and to each other.

  • Joy is serious work. Delight, play, laughter and fun are not incidental to the lab — they are part of the R&D. If it isn't joyful, we're doing something wrong.

07 · The Path Forward

Three horizons

This lab grows organically, rooted in a location and a community. Here is how we imagine the journey from seed to flourishing ecosystem.

Horizon 1 · Near

The Seed Lab

Register the association. Convene the founding circle in Genthod and Versoix. Run the first open water sessions. Begin the repair ateliers. Establish the material palette. Open the conversation with Biomimicry Switzerland and EPFL. Document everything.

Horizon 2 · Mid

The Dojo

A physical home in Nyon or Rolle — workshop, testing space, lakeside access. Subscription model running. Regular coaching, open ateliers, wellbeing sessions. Visiting designers and biologists. First school partnerships. A self-sustaining community.

Horizon 3 · Far

The Living Network

Multiple labs, bioregionally distinct, openly connected. A shared design commons. A global community of pump foil practitioners and biomimetic makers. The practice spreads — on every lake, every shoreline — carried by people who found something they didn't know they needed.

08 · Open Questions

What we are still discovering

This vision document is deliberately alive. The following questions are generative — they will shape the lab as it grows, answered by the community that builds it.

  • Where is the founding home? Nyon, Rolle, Morges — or somewhere else on the Léman shore? What commune will welcome a dojo on its waterfront?

  • Who are the founding circle? What mix of riders, makers, ecologists, educators and community connectors will form the first cohort at Versoix?

  • How do we document the wellbeing dimension? Can we partner with researchers to measure what practitioners are already feeling — the cortisol drop, the nervous system restoration, the emotional release?

  • What is the governance model? Community-owned cooperative? Swiss association? What structures best protect openness and longevity for the people who belong to it?

  • Which species teach us first? The great crested grebe? The pike? The fera? The eel? What are the master swimmers of the Léman, and what can each one teach a foil designer?

  • How does safety evolve with the community? What protective innovations — in equipment, in culture, in protocol — can we develop and share with the wider foiling world?

  • Who else should be here? If this speaks to you — if you run, or make things, or love this lake, or simply want to belong to something that matters — the door is open. Come and find us.
