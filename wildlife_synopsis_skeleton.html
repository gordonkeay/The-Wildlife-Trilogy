Book Two of the Wildlife Trilogy

Wildlife

Finding My Way Home

Gordon Keay
Genthod · Lac Léman · 2026
The Book

Synopsis

Wildlife is a memoir about a boy who was thrown into the world before he was ready and spent forty five years finding his way back. It is told in the voice of the man he became — on the shore of Lac Léman, at a desk by candlelight in the small hours before anyone else is awake — pulling up the memory chain link by link from the bottom of the ocean.

The structure is fluid, associative, dreamlike — the way memory actually works. Not chronological but tidal. The lake pulls something up. A smell, an image, a body sensation. And the past arrives not in order but in meaning. The chapter about the pearl farm in Broome arrives when the man is thinking about beauty lost in nonchalance. The chapter about the Newcastle cell arrives when he's thinking about the compass that never wavered. Each memory finds its moment when the moment is ready to receive it.

The book begins with a man writing a letter to his eight year old self — the boy who stood in front of a trunk with his name sewn into every sock, including the socks, and did not understand what he had done wrong. It ends with the same man finding the answer to the question that boy was asking: home was never a place. It was the self before the severance. And the pearl — formed in darkness under pressure over forty five years of wildlife — has been there all along.

Wildlife is the love letter Gordon Keay needed to receive at eighteen and never did. It is also the letter he is writing to his son at thirty and to every man who was sent away before he was ready. And — most privately, most essentially — it is the letter he is finally writing to himself.

"Just trying to find your way home after this wildlife.

I blame no one. I am born of a million moments.
I just wanted to find my way home.
And thanks to her, to you, I did."

How It Moves

The Structure

The Lake Pulls It Up. Each chapter opens in the present — the man on the dock, or at the desk, or walking with the dog, or lying awake at 3am. Something in the present moment — a smell, a sensation, a word, an image in the water — opens a door into the past. The memory arrives fully and is tended with the mythic lens. Then the present returns.

Three Registers. Wildlife moves between three voices without announcing the shifts. The raw autobiographical voice — gritty, physical, honest, Grimsby in the grain. The mythic voice — the teller who steps forward at the threshold moments, who circles the image until it gives up everything it has. And the direct voice — the man speaking straight to the eight year old in the room, to Rupert, to Danica, to the reader who recognises themselves.

Four Candles. The vigil at the heart of the book. The eight year old. The masculine wound. The mother. Gordana. Four flames for four retrievals. The structure spirals around these four like water around a stone.

The Chapters

Skeleton Outline

Written Opening
The Letter
A man writes to his eight year old self
The desk. The candle. The small hours. A man who has been writing in the dark for weeks finally knows who he's been writing to all along. The letter begins. Not the polished version — the real one. It wasn't your fault. You were loved. You mattered. You still do. Everything that follows is the proof.
"This book is for you Gordon, at eight years old. You with your trunk filled with clothes with your name sewn into every item, including the socks."
Source: The Vigil — 27th March 2026
Written Chapter One
Before the Wildlife
The kingdom before the severance
Grimsby. The yellow BMX with blue wheels. Scamper the white jack russell. The mustard fields. The butterflies so thick on the bush it looked ready to collapse. The gooseberries behind the bathtub. The long doughnuts with cream. Boris Becker dives on the lawn. The boy who practiced scuba diving in an orange dinghy with an icy hosepipe. The kingdom that was about to be taken.
"I had forgotten that boy. The eight year old me who loved running in the fields with his white jack russell Scamper."
Source: The Vigil — 27th March 2026
Written Chapter Two
The Severance
Boarding school at eight — the wound at the origin of everything
The trunk. The name tags sewn into every sock. The Peak District cold. The goodbye to Scamper — the first love who never hurt on the deep level. The lesson learned: children should be seen and not heard. The attempted escape — the cricket pavilion, the steep hillside, the hitchhiking, the punishment. The mutation. The tongue clipped. The compass smashed before it was learned.
"Why did they send you away? You always asked. Never knew. I'm so sorry."
Source: The Vigil, Voice Recordings, Dream-Time Diaries
Forming Chapter Three
The Great Grandfather's Portrait
The inheritance nobody spoke of
The portrait that hung on the wall and was never explained. The mayor of Grimsby. The church founder. The harsh man whose severity moved through the generations — grandfather to father to son. The silence that was its own inheritance. The cycle of repression. And the discovery at a funeral — too late, almost too late — that there was a man in the bloodline who stood up, built something, had a voice. The great grandson trying to understand what got passed down and what got lost.
"Why didn't you talk about him? I wonder what grandpa had to go through as a little boy."
Source: Dream-Time Diaries, Voice Recordings — parents needed
Forming Chapter Four
The Ogre's Castle
The stepfather, the rage, and the forging of the warrior
The man who tore the world apart. The cortisol bath night after night. Growing hard to survive a hard environment. The boxing bag named Bryan. The dunking of rugby players twice his size. The armour forged in anger that would take decades to remove. And the slow forgiveness that arrived not for the ogre's sake but for his own — the hatred no longer serving, the energy better used.
"You became the central focus for my rage and I pulverised a boxing bag named Bryan."
Source: The Vigil — 27th March 2026
Written Chapter Five
Mr Malcolm's Classroom
The first time the voice was heard
The history class. The Shakespeare read aloud. The wild bard of a man who takes up enormous space in tweed and wombat boots who doesn't ask permission. The voice praised — genuinely, specifically, by someone with no reason to be kind. Henry the Eighth offered. The shyness. The no thank you. The defining refusal that echoed for forty years. What it cost to back away from the spotlight when the spotlight was finally right.
"He was the man who takes up space like Hagrid, who doesn't ask permission, who inhabits a huge resonant vessel of a body."
Source: Dream-Time Diaries — 28th March 2026
Forming Chapter Six
The Wild Ones
Stoke, the clubs, chemicals, and finding a frequency
Sixteen years old. The nightclubs in Stoke. Chemicals helping him feel what he couldn't feel sober. Falling in thick as thieves with the most likely to be expelled. The rebel resonance. The compass that never wavered about right and wrong even in the middle of the chemical years. The shoplifting with Rupert. The police. The poem. The lies. Lucky to have survived his childhood.
"I always was attracted to the wild ones, those most likely to be expelled from polite society."
Source: Voice Recordings, Writing Practice Journal
Written Chapter Seven
The Night in the Cell
Newcastle — the secret kept for decades
The club. The bouncer. The wrestling. The car to the station. The night in the cell. The morning walk out into the light. The secret kept from everyone. Not from shame exactly — from the knowledge that nobody in his life could hold the full complexity of who he was. The man who reads Paracelsus by candlelight also spent a night in a Newcastle cell because his compass told him to act. Both true. Neither cancels the other.
"I always knew right from wrong. Like a compass that never wavered. Injustice sickened me."
Source: Voice Recordings — March 2026
Written Chapter Eight
Two Boys in Water
Rupert and Gordon — the sinker and the surface fighter
Two pools. Two countries. Two temperaments revealed at the threshold between life and death. Rupert who sank with curiosity and turned blue. Gordon who thrashed to the surface, momentum carrying him up. The sinker and the surface fighter. The deep thinker and the gladiator of the light. The wild twin born twenty moons before. And the question that runs through everything: what does each one carry that the other needs?
"Rupert always was a deep thinker, drawn to the bottom of things. I came to it later."
Source: Dream-Time Diaries — 27th March 2026, Voice Recordings
Written Chapter Nine
The Great Escape
The homing pigeon — and the pattern that runs through everything
The farm at twelve. Ben Wargate. The walk out without a word. The photographs taken on a road driven once. Every turning memorised. Steve McQueen throwing pebbles into a pot. The boy sitting on the roadside, proud of himself, waiting to be found. The pattern that would repeat — Broome, the boarding school escape, the boat, the countries, the marriages — always the homing pigeon navigating back to something he couldn't yet name.
"I made a great escape and I almost got home and I was only about eleven or twelve."
Source: Voice Recording — March 2026
Forming Chapter Ten
The Venice Beach Candle
The first practice — gratitude by firelight before Fiji
The fortune teller. The candle practice given to a young man about to cross the Pacific. Two months of nightly gratitude by firelight. The instrument beginning to tune. The first encounter with the practice that would become the dream-time vigils twenty years later. The Nudibranch lens before he had the word for it. Paying attention as the gateway drug to the soul.
Source: Writing Practice Journal, Archeus Draft
Written Chapter Eleven
The Reef
Fiji — the worm, the coral, the edge of the deep
The reef in Fiji and the dark fathomless drop beyond it. The worm taken in through the food and the water, slowly hollowing him out. The photographs of a gaunt hollow-cheeked man who didn't know he was being diminished from the inside. The nudibranchs. The whale sharks. The marine conservation work. And the vertigo of swimming to the edge of the reef and looking down into the dark where the monsters roam.
"I was imprisoned, emaciated, hollow cheeked, gaunt. Unable to tap into the joy. The worm was taking over."
Source: Dream-Time Diaries — 26th March 2026
Written Chapter Twelve
The Pearl Farm
Broome — beauty lost and the skipper who said he'd protect me
The dawn departure. The boobies flying, the sea turtles lazily paddling, the sea snakes coming up for air. The dangerous men with that particular look in their eyes. The hose. The Oakleys poised on the surface for one impossible second before the engine blades and the ocean decided. The skipper who said he'd protect him. The mango tree. The lizards running on their back legs. The camp full of small ridiculous miracles.
"I saw beauty lost in such nonchalance and it changed me."

Forming Chapter Thirteen
Dolores — The Huntress
Cranfield — the initiation, the pearl pierced, the naive cuckold
The woman who came into classrooms carefully at the end, leather and green, not dipping down to their level. The salsa, the beer, the pool. The fairy tale written to woo her. The old comfortable magic not working. The jealousy that made him wild. The fighting for love the way he'd never fought before. The trip to Slovenia delivering the furs. The fire salamander under the grape vines. And the Vipassana cushion in Hereford where the pieces were gathered up.
"She who spoke the old language. Does a goddess need an excuse to take a mortal for her pleasure?"

Forming Chapter Fourteen
The Circle at Schumacher
Dartmoor — the first time the voice was heard in public
Schumacher College and the belly of the beast where Small is Beautiful. The singing coach. The circle. The standing up and shaking — trauma pouring out. The longing for tigers and the weight of trying to fix a broken planet. And then — the people listening. Not laughing. Not filling the room with their own noise. Actually listening. The first time in public that his words mattered. The rehearsal for everything that followed.
"I felt welcome for one of the first times in my life. I felt like I was contributing. I felt respected."

Forming Chapter Fifteen
Tinder — Tender
Danica — the container, the patron saint, the gift
The digital courtship. The first date and the shared frequency of two lost souls. The wooing with home-made quiche and hand-rolled croissants and freshly baked bread. The container she gave him to grow inside. The French roots growing under her wing. Oliver born in the ice storm — the winter boy. Maya arriving like a rocket into warm water — the summer girl. The cancer. The ring breaking. The letter. The cupboard. Love joy and cuddles. The love that stayed.
"Tender from tinder. She gave me space to grow and I ran with it."

Arriving Chapter Sixteen
The Alcohol Years
Five years sober — and what the drinking was doing
What alcohol was for — the social lubrication for a man who couldn't bond with men any other way. The numbing of the things that couldn't be named. The Stella drinking clubs and their particular ugliness. The bottles on the apartment floor in Nottingham, calling anyone who would listen in a drunken confession. The decision not to become his father. The five years of sobriety that made the dream-time diaries possible. The instrument cleaned.
"I didn't want to become my father. I fought and I won. Five years sober."

Arriving Chapter Seventeen
Gordana
From effeminate to effervescent — the feminine retrieved
Danica in America. The quiet house. The underwear on the wedding day that turned him on. The first dress. The lace. Looking in the mirror and seeing someone. Feeling sexy for the first time at forty five. Feeling elegant. The bisexuality acknowledged. The shame examined and put down. The letter to Danica. The ring breaking. The cupboard. And then — Gordana coming over the castle wall. Bracingly. On her own terms. Little bubbles of energy throbbing in the body. From effeminate to effervescent.
"She was not a quiet princess in the tower. She came out bracingly. She was hungry and alive."

Arriving Chapter Eighteen
The Mother Wound
The fourth candle — the most complicated love
The mother who showed him art and food and cloth making and gardening. Who let him run free. And then the boarding school. And then the ogre. The frequency that never quite matched. The sense of Rupert being seen more clearly. The forgiveness extended to the stepfather but not yet fully to her. The fourth candle lit instinctively one morning. The most complex grief of all — the wound that lives inside the love. The work still being done.
"I wonder if the mother wound is deeper and I haven't totally healed from that."

Written Closing Chapter
The Pearl in Your Hand
Finding home
The naked chef who peeled another layer off and another until the onion disappeared. The man who came to the dock and failed a thousand times and got up a thousand and one. The vigil. The four candles. The letter to the eight year old. The retrieval. The cycle of inherited trauma ending here. And home — not Lac Léman, not Geneva, not any place at all. Home in Danica's hand holding the pearl. Home in Maya skipping and singing. Home in Oliver the winter boy asleep in the next room. Home found at last.
"From your husband, the naked chef, who peeled another layer off and another until the onion disappeared and a pearl lay in your hand. I found home."

The Honest Picture

Where You Actually Stand

Of twenty chapters plus opening and closing, roughly ten have substantial written material already existing in the dream-time diaries, voice recordings and writing practice journal. Four are forming — the material is there but needs excavating and shaping. Three are actively arriving as the process continues. Two are awaiting future work — the parent recordings and the Rupert conversation.

The closing chapter is already written. The opening is already written. The spine of the book exists. What remains is the middle — which is exactly where the life happened and where the most important work is.

This is not a book that needs to be invented. It needs to be excavated. The ore is in the ground. The dream-time diaries are the mining operation. Schmieta is the geological survey. The writing group in Geneva is the community around the work. And the shaping — the editing, the tending, the finding of the true voice in each chapter — that happens one passage at a time.
