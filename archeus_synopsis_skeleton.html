Book One of the Wildlife Trilogy

ARCHEUS

A Journal of the Lake & What It Taught Me

"The art of healing comes from nature, not from the physician. Therefore the physician must start from nature, with an open mind." — Paracelsus, 1493–1541
The Book

Synopsis

Archeus is a philosophical novel in the form of a journal. A man — known only as Archeus — comes to the shore of Lac Léman broken, depleted, and in need of something he cannot name. He begins to keep a journal. Over the course of weeks he encounters a series of teachers: a grebe that reads water with complete attention, an eel that moves through current with perfect efficiency, a mysterious figure on the water who appears to be flying, a Paracelsus found on a forgotten shelf, a boathouse community of makers and healers and dreamers. Through these encounters — and through the practice of pump foiling that becomes the laboratory for all of it — he begins to understand what the Archeus actually is: the inner architect, the vital healing intelligence that has always known how to restore what has been broken, if only we stop interfering with it.

The novel is structured as a journal with three temporal layers moving simultaneously. The present — Archeus on the shore, at the dock, in the boathouse, writing by evening light. The past — flashbacks to the Pacific years, the reef, the nudibranch, the candle practice given by a fortune teller in Venice Beach. And the universal — the Paracelsus epigraphs that open each chapter, which are invented in his spirit rather than transcribed from his work, speaking across five centuries to the questions the journal is asking.

The novel ends not with arrival but with opening. The boathouse is becoming something. The foundation is being called into being. The lake is still teaching. And the reader — whoever recognises the frequency — is invited to the shore.

"He who knows nothing, loves nothing. He who understands also loves, notices, sees.
The more knowledge is inherent in a thing, the greater the love.

The lake is the teacher. We are all still the students."

Structure

Three Temporal Layers

Archeus moves between three registers without announcing the shifts. The reader learns to feel the difference between them the way the grebe learns to feel the difference between surface and depth.

The Present
Archeus at the lake. The dawn walks. The grebe. The dock. The boathouse. The journal entries written in real time. The man in the process of learning.
The Past
The Pacific years. Venice Beach. The reef. The nudibranch. The candle practice. The retreat. The flashbacks that arrive when the lake pulls them up.
The Universal
Paracelsus. The epigraphs opening each chapter. The 500 year old voice speaking across time to the exact question being asked. Invented in his spirit.
· · ·

The honest picture of where Archeus stands. This is the most complete of the three books. A full draft exists — approximately 23,000 words across seventeen chapters. The architecture is strong. The Paracelsus thread works. The nature observation passages are already in your voice. The boathouse community is alive.

What it needs is your true voice breathed into it chapter by chapter. The current draft was built collaboratively with Claude and reads, in places, as elegant construction rather than lived experience. The journal asides and observation boxes are often the most alive sections — they carry your specific sensory intelligence. The main journal entries sometimes move too smoothly, too polished, too far from the grit that makes Wildlife and Paramanta unmistakably yours.

The work is not rewriting from scratch. It is moving through each chapter and asking — where is my actual voice in here? Where do I recognise myself? Then finding the raw material that belongs there and doing what we did with Paramanta — building from the inside out using your own words as the ore.

The Chapters

Skeleton Outline

Working Before Everything — I
The Candle
Venice Beach — the fortune teller — the practice that started everything
A young man on a crowded boulevard in California. A fortune teller who sees something in his palm and offers not a prophecy but a practice. A candle and a single instruction: find the small things. Two months of nightly gratitude by firelight on a Pacific island. The origin of the dream-time vigils twenty years before he understood what he was building. The candle was first.
"She gave me the evening practice. But the evening practice gave me the days."
Already written and working. The connection to the dream-time diaries needs to be made explicit — this is the same practice Gordon has been doing all along.
Working Before Everything — II
The Nudibranch
The Pacific reef — eighteen metres — the teacher that taught attention
The reef and its hierarchy of wonder — from the manta and the eagle ray and the humphead wrasse down to the three centimetre sea slug with lungs on the outside. The lesson that the big things take your attention but the small things require you to give it. The shift from conservationist moving too fast to diver learning to stop. The hour spent at one section of reef wall counting thirty species. The nudibranch as the origin of the lens.
"The things we pay attention to become the beauty we care most about."
Strong as written. The connection to Gordon's own nudibranch experience in the underworld passage needs threading through.
Needs Voice Before Everything — III
Solve et Coagula
The retreat — the dissolution — the body releasing what the mind refused
Six days of silence on the shore of Lac Léman. Coming broken without admitting the breaking. On the sixth day — something giving way. Energy moving through the body like current through water. The trembling, the vibration, the releases that felt like relief. The body storing what the mind could not process. The fascial matrix as archive of everything carried without metabolising. The fire made by hand from flint and grass. The lake seen properly for the first time. Tomorrow he will walk to the shore.
"Solve et coagula. You cannot begin until you have first come apart."
This chapter needs Gordon's voice most urgently. The retreat experience in the current draft is well constructed but generic. The raw material from the dream-time diaries — the vigil, the body's releases, the stored grief — belongs here. This is where the personal breaks through into the universal.
Working Prologue
The First Entry
Archeus begins his journal — the grebe is the reason
The man introduces himself — Archeus, a man who pushed too hard and arrived at a place where pushing was no longer possible. He is starting this journal because something happened at the lake that he does not want to lose. He watched a bird dive. That is all. And something in him went very quiet. Clean and strong. The opening is properly yours — it has the right quality of restraint. Keep as is.
Needs Voice Chapter One
The Grebe
The first teacher — reading water with complete attention
The dawn walks before light. The lake in early morning as a different substance. The grebe found on the third morning — its quality of zero waste, the moment before the dive, the stillness that becomes more still, the reading of the water beneath. The finding of the Paracelsus on the lowest shelf of the study that afternoon. The passage about the Book of Nature written in the language of the things themselves. The grebe and Paracelsus teaching the same thing from opposite ends of five centuries.
"The grebe reads it fluently. I am still learning the alphabet."
The dawn observation passages are extraordinary and already in your voice — the colours of the lake, the birdsong, the edges as the most interesting places. The main journal entries need more of this physical specificity throughout. The Paracelsus discovery works well. Expand the grebe's teaching — what does the grebe know about the threshold between two worlds?
Needs Voice Chapter Two
The Eel
Undulation — effort distributed through the whole
The eel seen at the edge of the reeds. The quality of its movement — not the thrust of something pushing through resistance but the wave that finds the path of least resistance through every medium it encounters simultaneously. Paracelsus on the body's wisdom. The eel as the physical demonstration of what the Archeus is — the intelligence that distributes effort perfectly, that wastes nothing, that moves through the world by reading it rather than fighting it. The lesson that will arrive again in the foil and the pump.
"The eel taught me to feel. The most powerful force travels not through the straightest line but through the most intelligent curve."
This chapter exists in the closing entry summary but needs its full scene. The eel is one of the central teachers. Write the encounter in full — where you were standing, what the light was doing, how long you watched. The physical specificity of the eel's movement needs the same quality of attention as the grebe observation.
Working Chapter Three
The Figure on the Water
The first sighting — the man who appeared to be flying
Walking the shore in the golden afternoon light. A figure on the open water — standing, moving, flying. The attempt to parse what he is seeing. Not paddling, not sailing, not propelled by anything external. The field observation recorded immediately at the shore with cold hands. The Paracelsus falling open to the passage about the art of allowing. The decision: I am going to find him. Not to ask him to teach me. First, to understand what it is I watched him do. To read it before I try to speak it.
"This is what it looks like when a human being learns to read the Book of Nature fluently."
One of the strongest chapters. The field observation box is excellent — precise, physical, alive. The journal aside at the end is too long and slightly over-explains. Trust the image. Cut the explanation by half and let the recognition land without commentary.
Needs Voice Chapter Four
The Man at the Shore
The meeting — the teacher found — the first conversation
Three mornings at the headland finding nothing. The fourth morning — the man crouched at the shore working on the foil. The Paracelsus caught by a gust and caught by the man's hand. The first exchange — different century, same water. The long conversation about the practice — what effortlessness actually means, what it costs, what the body learns that the mind cannot think. The instruction to leave the Paracelsus on the shore when you get in the water. Tomorrow he will go early.
"The effort becomes invisible. Even to yourself. That is what you are working toward — the moment when you stop feeling the work and start feeling the water."
The dialogue works but the man needs more of your specific sensory vocabulary. His face, his hands on the foil surface, the quality of his silence. The instruction to leave the Paracelsus on the shore is the chapter's best moment — let it breathe more.
Needs Voice Chapter Five
On cherche le glisse
The thousand falls — the practice itself
Weeks five through eight at the dock. One thousand falls honestly reported. The dock start described in full — drop, rebound, jump, landing like a cat. The instruction that arrives on the thirtieth day: you go where you look. Look down, go down. Lift the head, open to the world in front, trust the thousand repetitions. The moment when the ego is put down and the Archeus permitted to do what it always knew. Three seconds of glide. The mantra arrives: on cherche le glisse. Not the destination. The searching.
"I had been fighting my own Archeus for twenty-three days. I finally stepped aside. And it knew exactly what to do."
This chapter has the strongest connection to your actual writing — the dock start passages from the journal are almost verbatim in places. This is where the book most clearly becomes yours. Needs the rainy morning sessions, the wind on the chop, the laughing alone on the water. The worm analogy from the dream-time diaries — learning the body's intelligence from inside — belongs here.

The connection to Gordon's own wound becoming his material is the deepest thread of the whole trilogy. The quagga beach passage from the dream-time diaries belongs here.
Needs Voice Chapter Seven
The Boathouse
The workshop — the wing blank — the knowledge in the hands
A rainy day. The man at the door: come, there is nothing to learn on the water today, today we go to the workshop. The boathouse among the larch and ash — linseed oil, timber shavings, the smell of something being made. The wing blank on the trestle — pale wood, roughly shaped, the form implied rather than imposed. Sandpaper and the instruction: always with the grain, slowly — the water will feel everything. The particular aching of investment rather than extraction. The recognition of a space organised entirely around making.
"The work changes the worker. This is the great secret. This is what the alchemists were always trying to say."
The boathouse chapter identified earlier as most urgently needing your voice. The workshop observation box is already excellent. What's missing is your specific making history — the bread, the fabric at Schumacher, the foil you planned with basalt and flax. The man at the workbench needs more of your Knock on Wood studio sensibility. This is where the Biomimetic Pump Foil Lab concept belongs in fictional form.
Needs Voice Chapter Eight
The Bread
Alchemy in the kitchen — solve et coagula made edible
An early morning. The sourdough starter fed and set to prove. Paracelsus's tria prima applied to bread — sulphur the wild yeast, mercury the water, salt the flour. The fire as the fourth force. The transformation that goes both ways: the bread becomes bread and the baker becomes something they could not have become without the making. The practice of gathering small joys through the day to bring to the flame at night. The bread making me as I make it. The cosmic dance made edible.
"The bread makes me as I bake it. We dance in a circularity. This is alchemy. Not the making of gold from lead — the making of a self from the faithful application of attention, heat, and time."
This chapter already contains your voice most fully — the bread essay is explicitly yours, barely disguised. Expand it with the specific sensory experience of baking gluten free bread for a family with allergies, the particular satisfaction of feeding people who need careful feeding. The connection between bread and the foil wing — both shaped by hand, both requiring the right conditions to rise — is the chapter's philosophical spine.
Needs Rewrite Chapter Nine
The Others
The community gathering — the whole truth distributed
The people arriving without announcement at the shore and the boathouse. The woman who had been ill. The boy on his bicycle before school. The cabinet maker with forty years of hand knowledge who arrives and sits down and begins working without introduction. The man's teaching: the whole truth is distributed. No single source holds it. You have to move through the world to find it. The boy's question: what do you become? The man's answer: come back tomorrow and we will find out together.
"This is how it was always supposed to happen. Many people, each bringing what they have, each receiving what they need. The knowledge doesn't live in any of us. It lives between us."
The gathering is beautifully conceived but the characters are too archetypal — the woman, the boy, the cabinet maker are types rather than people. They need specific details, specific voices, specific knowledge only they carry. Think of Darrell Tatum and Bob the panel beater — people remembered with total specificity. Give these people that quality. Who are they really?

The Work Ahead

How to Breathe Your Voice Into It

The method is the same as we used for Paramanta. You go through each chapter and ask three questions. Where do I recognise myself? Where does it sound like someone else's idea of me? Where is the specific sensory truth missing?

Then you go to the raw material — the dream-time diaries, the voice recordings, the journal entries — and find the passages that belong there. The quagga beach crunching underfoot. The pollen that started attacking at thirty and hasn't stopped. The postman at 4am in the dead of winter. The specific smell of the boathouse. The way the lake looks at that exact shade between grey and silver before the warmth arrives.

The architecture doesn't need to change. The Paracelsus thread works. The three temporal layers work. The chapter titles are right. What needs to change is the texture — the specific, irreplaceable, only-Gordon sensory intelligence that makes the difference between a beautifully constructed journal and a living one.

Start with the Boathouse chapter. That's the one you identified as most urgently needing your voice. One chapter at a time. The same way the wing was made — slowly, with the grain, the water feeling everything.
